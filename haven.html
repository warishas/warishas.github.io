Safe Haven


During an ongoing crisis, some refugees have settled in Seattle. Aid to ease the transition is available but scattered and hard to find. Safe Haven aims to bridge the disconnect between resources and people who need them.

Introduction


When presented with a project theme of “back to whatever this is,” which explores technology in the context of the “new normal,” our team considered how we can help an underserved community growing in number. Due to course contraints, my team focused our user-centered design on people we had access to: those who have heard about the Afghan refugee crisis on the news and want to help but don’t know where to start .

Design Question


How might we increase a sense of belonging and community building amongst Afghan refugees and the larger Seattle community to improve their resettlement experiences?

User Research


We began the project by conducting semi-structured interviews coupled with surveys with adults who are aware of the refugee crisis and have an interest in volunteering to understand their time, and involvement, and transportation concerns as it relates to organizations supporting incoming refugees. We also conducted discovery interviews with volunteers and caseworkers from refugee relief organizations such as International Rescue Committee (IRC) and Jewish Family Services (JFS) to participate in discovery interviews before conducting a Competative Analysis to find existing solutions within this space.

While job boards and volunteer portals exist for official nonprofits, those who have gotten involved did so after a call to action from someone in their network. Our target audience consists of people willing to help incoming refugees but are not actively looking for opportunities nor are they connected with the refugee community.


Pain Points


Combining findings from multiple user research methods using an affinity diagram, my team broke down findings into topics to select the scope of our project. After deciding to focus on the issue of low awareness around opportunities, we organized our stakeholders into two groups: potential volunteers and existing organizations. We found:

  • Resources for refugees are out there but information about them is not readily available . There needs to be some kind of list of resources for people to tap into.
    - interview with recent refugee (former USA aid worker in Afghanistan)
  • Young and early career adults have heard about the Afghan refugee crisis and are open to getting involved in settlement efforts but are uninformed about opportunities to help.
    - 61.5% of survey respondents
  • Organizations need community partners and donors to provide financial support in order to continue running operations
    - discovery interview


Synthesis


I created 2 personas that embodied the archetypes of our user groups. I then mapped out the user journey of our primary persona, Casey, including her thoughts and feelings surrounding news coverage about the crisis in Afghanistan. From this, I was able to empathize with Casey's schedule and where she can offer support.

Problem Statement


Using the research and synthesis as a guideline, we summarized our findings with the following problem statement and intentions for our solution: How might we for behavior change and change will into action for those hearing about the Afghan refugee crisis and open to offering support?

Ideation


With the design requirements in mind, we began to ideate on potential solutions through sketches and storyboards.


We decided that while a convienient solution, an app does not reach our target audience without strong marketing. Knowing that we wanted to encourage a behavior change, we considered how a kiosk at a bus stop could help people evalute their opportunities while they wait for their ride


We also bucketed the design requirements as feature components on the information architecture (IA) to form the backbone of our kiosk experience.

Reflection


Early on in the quarter, the team faced a choice between two possible projects: one would be straightforward, with easy access to target users and a fairly clear array of possible design solutions. The other became Safe Haven. At the outset, we were hesitant to pursue this project because of the potential difficulty of engaging stakeholders and the uncertainty over what a possible design solution might look like. But when it came time to choose, we were unanimous: we all wanted to pursue a purposeful, impact-driven project.

While not without its challenges, choosing this path was immensely rewarding, both in the experiences it led to and the lessons learned from those experiences. Among those lessons, maybe the most important was that the value of human-centered design is not just in the creation of more appropriate and usable design solutions that result from the involvement of users, but in the generative power of design as a process to bring together diverse stakeholders in pursuit of more inspiring and inclusive futures.
